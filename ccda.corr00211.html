Charles W. Chesnutt to Horace Traubel, 6 November 1905

CHAS. W. CHESNUTT

1005 WILLIAMSON BUILDING

CLEVELAND, O.

Cleveland, November 6, 1905.



Horace Traubel, Esq.,

1624 Walnut Street,

Philadelphia, Pa.



My dear Mr. Traubel:— *

On September 16th last I addressed you a letter, in which I enclosed a money order for $2.25, for a copy of the popular edition of Leaves of Grass" * and a copy of "Chants Communal." * As I have not received the books, I have been wondering if my letter miscarried or was perhaps mislaid after reaching its destination. If you will kindly let me know whether it came to hand or not, I shall be obliged.

Did you get a copy of "The Colonel's Dream"? * I am under the impression that I requested my publishers to send it, though I presume you are on their list. I hope the book, or at least my motive in writing, it will commend itself to you. I have just been reading your appreciation of Henry Irving * it is very fine and very just. The world is better because he lived.



Sincerely yours,

Chas. W. Chesnutt

sent to

second

Copy Chants

noon



Notes:

Horace Traubel (19 December 1858-9 September 1919)

Horace Traubel was an American poet, essayist, and author. Traubel was also a dedicated Socialist, and one of the founders of the socialist weekly newspaper The Worker. He is best known for being Whitman's literary executor and author of a nine-volume biography of Whitman's final four years (1888-1892), entitled Walt Whitman in Camden.

Leaves of Grass

Leaves of Grass is Walt Whitman's most renowned work; seven revised editions of the collection in Whitman's lifetime.

The "popular edition" that Chesnutt refers to is likely the Deathbed Edition of Leaves of Grass, published just two months before Whitman's death. In a statement announcing the publication of this edition, Whitman claimed that Leaves of Grass was finally complete.

Chant's Communal

Chant's Communalwas a collection of many of the essays that Horace Traubel wrote for The Worker, a weekly socialist newspaper that he helped found.

The Colonel's Dream

The Colonel's Dream (1905) was Chesnutt's final novel. The book gained little critical attention and didn't sell well—a reality that stood in opposition to the book's dedication: "to bring the forces of enlightenment to bear upon the vexed problems which harass the South." The Colonel's Dream tells the story of Colonel French, a successful New York businessman and recent widow who returns to his Southern birthplace, Clarendon, with his son, Phil. French attempts to advance Clarendon's social and economic progress for the modern era, hoping to overcome its prejudices, rigid social divisions, and corruptions and finds a nemesis in the wealthy convict labor contractor, William Fetters. Ultimately, French underestimates the entrenched racism of the town, and despite his best efforts, Clarendon appears wholly unchanged.



Notes:

Henry Irving (6 February 1838-13 October 1905)

Sir Henry Irving was a renowned English actor and manager at the Lyceum Theater, where he eventually established his own Company. Throughout his lifetime, he starred in several shows, including Hamlet, The Bells and Queen Mary. Irving was one of the inspirations for the character of Count Dracula. Dracula's author, Bram Stoker, worked as the business manager for Irving at the Lyceum Theater starting in 1878.
