Charles W. Chesnutt to Horace Traubel, 16 September 1905

CHAS. W. CHESNUTT

1005 WILLIAMSON BUILDING

CLEVELAND, O.

Cleveland,September 16, 1905.



Horace Traubel, Esq.,

1624 Walnut Street,

Philadelphia, Pa.



My dear Mr. Traubel:— *

My daughter Helen * , who was visiting recently in Philadelphia, tells me that she came very near meeting you, but was called home by a telegram the day before she would have had that pleasure. She has given me a couple of photographs which I think Mr. Bolivar * handed to her, and for which I thank you very much. It is a pleasure to look upon even the counterfeit presentment of the face one of whom I have learned to esteem from his writings, and who at the same time has shown his appreciation of my own. I hope to meet you personally before very long.

I see from the Conservator * that the Walt Whitman * books can be obtained from you. I should like to have a copy of the popular edition of Leaves of Grass * , and a volume of your own^ Chants Communal * . I enclose money order for $2.25, which I hope includes sufficient for the postage.



Yours sincerely,

Chas. W. Chesnutt.



Notes:

Horace Traubel (19 December 1858-9 September 1919)

Horace Traubel was an American poet, essayist, and author. Traubel was also a dedicated Socialist, and one of the founders of the socialist weekly newspaper The Worker. He is best known for being Whitman's literary executor and author of a nine-volume biography of hitman's final four years (1888-1892), entitled Walt Whitman in Camden.



Notes:

Helen Maria Chesnutt (1880-1969)

Helen Maria Chesnutt was the daughter of Charles Chesnutt. After attending Smith College, she taught Latin at Central High School in Cleveland, Ohio for many years, including to Langston Hughes. She co-authored a Latin textbook, The Road to Latin (1932) and served on the executive committee of the American Philological Association in 1920. She also authored an influential biography of her father, entitled Charles Waddell Chesnutt, Pioneer of the Color Line.



Notes:

William C. Bolivar (1849-November 1914)

William Carl Bolivar was a collector of African-American authored texts. He also worked as a journalist, writing under the pseudonym "Pencil Pusher" in Philadelphia Tribune and served as a key member in Philadelphia's American Negro Historical Society.

The Conservator

The Conservator was a monthly newspaper founded and edited by Traubel for 30 years. The newspaper frequently reflected its editor's idiosyncratic philosophy and crusaded for libertarian principles.



Notes:

Walt Whitman (31 May 1898-)

Walt Whitman is America's most influential poet and a writer of global renown. His major work, Leaves of Grass, underwent six extremely different editions. He also wrote journalistic articles and fiction, while leaving behind notebooks, manuscript fragments, prose essays, letters, and marginalia.

Leaves of Grass

Leaves of Grass is Walt Whitman's most renowned work; seven revised editions of the collection in Whitman's lifetime.

Chant's Communal

Chant's Communalwas a collection of many of the essays that Horace Traubel wrote for The Worker, a weekly socialist newspaper that he helped found.
