I send you herewith the manuscript of a story, or perhaps I ought to say a sketch, entitled: "The March of Progress," * with the hope that you may be able to use it in the Century. * It does not pretend to "atmosphere": but it is a plain story of plain people, although I think that gratitude is a fine theme and not overworked.

My book "The Conjure Woman," * which is on Messrs. Houghton, Mifflin & Co.'s * Spring list, is doing very well, and has been invariably commended by the newspaper reviewers, and by some of the best of them at considerable length and in proncounced terms. The tales are different from this however, which is more on the order of "The Wife of His Youth" * , which you were good enough to read and give me your opinion about. If this story is unavailable, kindly return the manuscript at as early a date as convenient, and oblige



Yours very truly,

Chas. W. Chesnutt.

Mr. R.W. Gilder,

Editor "The Century Magazine,"

New York.



Notes:

Richard Watson Gilder (1844-1909)

Richard Watson Gilder was a poet and editor of the Century magazine. Нe worked as a reporter for the Newark Daily Advertiser and the Newark Morning Register before becoming editor of the magazine Hours At Home in 1869. When the latter was merged with the newly-founded Scribner's Monthly the following year, Gilder was made assistant editor under Josiah Gilbert Holland. Не succeeded Holland as editor-in-chief in 1881 and turned the publication, renamed The Century Illustrated Monthly Magazine, into the most renowned and influential general interest magazine in the United States ("Overview," Richard Watson Gilder papers, 1855-1916 Manuscripts and Archives Division, The New York Public Library, 1987: 1).

The March of Progress

The short story "March of Progress" was published in The Century in 1901, and it features a dramaticization of the problem facing a black schoolboard in Patesville, North Carolina in the 1880s (William L. Andrews, The Literary Career of Charles W. Chesnutt, Louisiana State University Press, 1999: 82).

The Century

The Century Magazine was an American magazine published by The Century Company that replaced Scribner's Monthly Magazine in 1881. The magazine published illustrated articles on history, science, and literature, sometimes publishing fiction as well. In 1930, it merged with The Forum.

The Conjure Woman

The Conjure Woman is a collection of short stories by Chesnutt published in 1899. All of the tales are framed with narration by a white Northerner, John, who has travelled to the South with his wife, Annie, looking for a place to live. He recounts Uncle Julius, a man who was formerly enslaved on the land that the couple is looking to purchase, telling John and Annie conjure tales.

Houghton, Mifflin & Company

Initally founded in 1832 as Ticknor and Fields, the company published texts by impressive literary authors, including Thoreau, Hawthorne, and Emerson. In 1878, Ticknor and Fields was struggling financially, and merged with Henry Oscar Houghton's publishing company, Riverside Press, to form Houghton, Osgood and Company. Osgood left the business two years later and Houghton, Millin & Co. was created. The company continued to primarily publish literary texts, deciding in 1891 to expand and also publish educational material.

The Wife of His Youth

After its publication in the Atlantic Monthly, the short story "The Wife of His Youth" became the title piece of his collection of nondialect works published in 1899, The Wife of His Youth and Other Stories of the Color Line. As the title suggests, each of the stories in the collection examine racial issues of the time.
